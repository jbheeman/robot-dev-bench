Stereo Calibration Guide

To extract accurate 3D poses, the system must understand exactly how your two cameras are positioned relative to each other. Follow these steps to calibrate your stereo setup.

1

Prepare the ChArUco Board

You need a standard ChArUco calibration pattern (a hybrid checkerboard with ArUco tags inside).

  • Run the generate_charuco.py script to create a high-res board, and print it on a stiff, completely flat surface (like foam board or a thick clipboard). A bent paper will ruin the calibration.
  • Ensure it is a 10x7 ChArUco board (10 squares wide, 7 squares tall).
  • Measure the exact size of one square edge in metres (e.g., 0.025m for a 2.5cm square).
  • Measure the exact size of one inner black ArUco marker edge in metres (e.g., 0.015m for a 1.5cm marker).
2

Position the Cameras

Set up your two cameras covering the area where the robot will be moving.

  • Place Camera 1 (Primary) and Camera 2 (Secondary) on sturdy tripods.
  • Make sure their fields of view overlap significantly.
  • Crucial: Once positioned, do not move, bump, or refocus the cameras! If they move even slightly, you must re-calibrate.
3

Record the Calibration Video

Simultaneously record a short video (about 10 to 30 seconds) on both cameras.

  • Hold the ChArUco board in the overlapping field of view so both cameras can clearly see it at the same time.
  • Slowly move the board up, down, left, right, close to the cameras, and far away.
  • Tilt the board slightly along the X, Y, and Z axes. Because of the ArUco tags, partial occlusion and glare is fine—as long as a few tags are visible, calibration will succeed!
  • Ensure there is no motion blur. Move smoothly.
4

Upload and Solve

Upload the two resulting .mp4 videos into the calibration dashboard.

  • The system will extract synchronized frames and hunt for the ChArUco markers to interpolate the sub-pixel corners.
  • It computes the intrinsic parameters (lens distortion) and extrinsic parameters (rotation and translation matrices between the cameras).
  • Once complete, you will see a 3D visualization of your camera setup. A Stereo RMS error of less than 1.0 is considered a good calibration.
